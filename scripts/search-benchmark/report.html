SearXNG pipeline vs. Parallel Search API

A head-to-head on the balance of speed and quality — our self-hosted web-research pipeline against Parallel's managed Search API, answered through DeepSeek V4 Flash on the live deployment.

Model under test deepseek-v4-flash Judge Qwen 3.6 40B (independent) Questions 20 across 5 tiers Runs 240 (20×4×3) Search cost $0.36 total

Bottom line

Parallel Search — Turbo tier — wins the speed/quality balance decisively. ~520 ms retrieval, 98% correct, $1 per 1k queries, and it absorbed 60 rapid-fire calls without flinching.

Our pipeline's answer quality is respectable when SearXNG is healthy (2.25/3 on the tier we could measure cleanly), but the run exposed a first-order reliability problem: SearXNG's free upstream engines rate-limit and time out under load, and the pipeline stopped returning results for most of the run. That fragility — not answer quality — is the real story on our side.

Even here, Parallel edges ours — and the gap is instructive. Three of four (Python 3.10, Postgres 5432, vitamin-C 90 mg) tied at a perfect 3.0. The whole difference is A1 — the GitHub Copilot Pro price: our pipeline did retrieve sources, but the answer brief it assembled didn't surface the "$10/month" figure, so Flash correctly refused to guess and scored 0. Parallel's LLM-optimized excerpts carried the price, so Flash answered it. That's a grounding/extraction gap, not a retrieval-count gap — a real, if narrow, quality signal that survives the rate-limit contamination. (n=4 questions — small; directional, not definitive.)

04 Why our pipeline fell over

The collapse was monotonic by run order — healthy at the start, dead by the end — which points at load, not content:

tier A  (runs   0–19)   non-empty 20/20   mean 2.10   ← SearXNG healthy
tier B  (runs  20–39)   non-empty  8/20   mean 1.20   ← degrading
tier C  (runs  40–59)   non-empty  1/20   mean 0.15
tier D  (runs  60–79)   non-empty  2/20   mean 0.15
tier D/E(runs  80–119)  non-empty  0/20   mean 0.00   ← fully starved

Root cause — SearXNG's engines, straight from its logs

The harness fired ~480 SearXNG queries in 22 minutes. Production traffic is paced by real users; this burst tripped the free upstream engines. Probing each engine on the box afterward:

This is a latent production risk independent of the Parallel decision: general search on this instance rides almost entirely on Brave, which suspends under modest load, while Google and DuckDuckGo are effectively dead from the box. It may already be causing silent "found nothing" answers for users.

05 Cost at volume

Parallel bills per request; SearXNG is a flat server cost. Turbo at $1/1k makes cost a near-non-factor — and that's before pricing in the ops burden and reliability risk of self-hosting.

No production web-search volume analytics exist yet, so these are scenarios. At any realistic scale, Turbo's cost is dominated by the value of dropping the SearXNG ops/reliability burden.

06 Recommendation

Adopt Parallel Search Turbo as the default retrieval path, and let the model escalate to Advanced for hard multi-hop questions where source ranking matters (Advanced's hit@3 was 92% vs Turbo's 50%). Rationale: same-or-better quality, 3–8× faster retrieval, trivial cost, and — the deciding factor — it doesn't fall over under load.

  • Turbo for the fast path (≈520 ms, $1/1k): the quick/navigational/freshness majority.
  • Advanced for deep research (≈2.3 s, $5/1k): multi-hop synthesis where ranking/coverage matters. Its excerpts also close the grounding gap that cost us A1.
  • Fix SearXNG regardless if it stays in the stack as a fallback: add Bing (already working) to the general category, or an API-keyed engine, and enable the limiter. Today it's a single-engine-of-failure.

07 Method & honest caveats

So you can weight the conclusions correctly:

  • Setup. 20 gold-verified questions (5 stress tiers), each answered by DeepSeek V4 Flash given only that provider's retrieved context, then graded 0–3 by an independent judge against the gold answer. Ran in-process on the live box against the real SearXNG + TEI reranker pipeline.
  • Judge. Qwen 3.6 40B (local) — chosen because the OpenAI key is out of quota and Fireworks is unauthorized; it's independent of DeepSeek, which avoids family bias, but it isn't a frontier grader. Facts here are unambiguous, so this is low-risk, but scores are judge-relative.
  • Ours quality sample is small (n=4). A clean 20-question run for our pipeline wasn't achievable this session — SearXNG's engines are rate-limited/down on the box. The Parallel figures are the robust ones; ours is directional.
  • Retrieval hit@3 undercounts us. It credits only the specific gold domains; a correct answer sourced elsewhere still scores a miss. Answer quality (the judge) is the ground truth, hit@3 is a secondary signal.
  • One box, one day. Latencies include the model call and this network path; treat them as relative, not absolute SLAs.
  • Next step to close the gap: re-run ours cleanly after adding a reliable SearXNG engine (or overnight with heavy pacing), to confirm whether the A1-style grounding gap generalizes.
